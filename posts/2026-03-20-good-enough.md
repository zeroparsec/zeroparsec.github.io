A nuclear reactor in the Gobi Desert reached criticality on 11 October 2023, . It was a modest machine — two megawatts of thermal output, the size of a small industrial facility, tucked into an arid industrial park in Wuwei, Gansu Province. The Chinese Academy of Sciences had been building toward this moment for twelve years, assembling nearly a hundred research institutions, developing new materials, training a generation of engineers in a technology that no other country was actively pursuing.

>Since achieving first criticality on October 11, 2023, the thorium molten salt reactor has been steadily generating heat through nuclear fission.
>
>— Li Qingnuan: _Deputy Director, Shanghai Institute of Applied Physics_

When the reactor held its first sustained reaction, no one outside the programme paid much attention. But the technology was invented in Tennessee in the 1950s. The man who built it was fired for refusing to stop believing in it.

A procurement decision made in 1949 — for a submarine, under military constraints, with a six-year deadline — still governs how most of the world generates nuclear power today.
Start with the submarine.

---

In February 1949, a driven, difficult Navy captain named Hyman Rickover assigned himself a problem: put a nuclear reactor inside a submarine hull and make it work by 1955. The constraint was physical. A submarine has a fixed beam — the USS Nautilus was designed around a hull no wider than twenty-eight feet. Whatever reactor went in it had to fit, had to be reliable, and had to be ready. Rickover was not a theorist. He was an engineer who treated every problem as a construction problem, and he had simultaneously embedded himself in both the Navy's nuclear programme and the Atomic Energy Commission's reactor development division, giving him institutional leverage that no one else had thought to acquire.

The design he chose used water as both coolant and moderator, kept under enormous pressure — roughly a hundred times atmospheric — to stop it boiling at reactor temperatures. It worked. The Nautilus sailed in 1955 and ran flawlessly. Nine years later, when the AEC needed to demonstrate civilian nuclear power, it chose the Rickover-Westinghouse reactor for Shippingport, Pennsylvania — the world's first commercial nuclear power plant. The decision was not made because pressurised water was the optimal design for generating electricity on a grid. It was made because Rickover already had a working organisation, existing contracts, and a proven reactor. The submarine became the template for the civilian industry, not because anyone decided it should, but because it was already there.

A pressurised water reactor is, by nature, a system under stress. The coolant runs at pressures that make any breach potentially explosive. Containing that possibility requires massive engineering — thick-walled pressure vessels, elaborate cooling redundancies, expensive containment domes. The system is safe when everything works. When something doesn't, the pressure is the problem. Three Mile Island. Fukushima. In both cases, the cooling system failed and the pressure did what pressure does.

At Oak Ridge National Laboratory in Tennessee, a physicist named Alvin Weinberg had spent the 1950s and 1960s developing a different answer. His reactor dissolved the uranium fuel directly into a mixture of molten fluoride salts. The salt ran at atmospheric pressure. If the system lost power, a plug of frozen salt at the base of the reactor melted and the fuel drained into a containment vessel, where it could no longer sustain a reaction. The fail-safe was in the physics, not the engineering workaround. Oak Ridge built and ran a working version from 1965 to 1969. It operated successfully. The proof of concept existed.

Weinberg spent the following years trying to convince the AEC to fund the next stage — a larger molten salt breeder reactor, building on what the experiment had proved. The Nixon administration had other priorities. Its chosen technology was the liquid metal fast breeder reactor, championed by Milton Shaw, the AEC's Director of Reactor Development, who pursued it with what Weinberg later described as Rickoverian dedication. Weinberg kept arguing for molten salt. In 1973, after eighteen years as Oak Ridge's director, he was told his time was up. He was fired for refusing to stop.

There was no further molten salt development in the United States through Nixon, Ford, or Reagan. A brief revival under Carter produced a reference design that researchers still cite today, then was shelved. The institutional knowledge scattered. The supply chains that would have been needed never formed. The light water reactor — Rickover's submarine solution — became the global standard, carrying with it the pressure systems, the waste profiles, and the meltdown scenarios that made civilian nuclear power politically toxic for a generation.

China reached criticality on its molten salt test reactor in October 2023. The programme was developed with technical assistance from the US Department of Energy, which helped fund it because the United States currently lacks the political will to build one domestically. The country that invented the technology is helping someone else prove it works, on a timeline set by someone else's priorities.

The inventor's country. The inventor's technology. Someone else's reactor.

What happened at Oak Ridge was not deliberate. No one decided that the better technology should lose. Rickover was solving a real problem under real constraints. The AEC chose what already worked. Nixon's administration had a preferred technology and a director who wouldn't stop disagreeing with it. Each decision, taken individually, was defensible. Taken together, they produced an outcome that no single person chose and that proved nearly impossible to undo.

Economists have a name for this. Brian Arthur, studying technology adoption in the 1980s, showed that early choices in competing technologies tend to become self-reinforcing — the more a technology is adopted, the more infrastructure forms around it, the more expensive switching becomes, until the original choice is effectively permanent regardless of its merits. Paul David, examining why the **QWERTY keyboard layout** outlasted every superior alternative, called this path dependence: the past constrains the possible, not through anyone's intention, but through the compounding logic of sunk costs, network effects, and switching costs accumulating silently over time.

The mechanism has a shape. A technology gets chosen early, for reasons that make sense at the time. Infrastructure forms around it — supply chains, trained workforces, regulatory frameworks, contractor relationships. Each layer of infrastructure raises the cost of switching to an alternative. The alternative has to be not merely better, but better by enough to justify dismantling everything that has already been built. It rarely is.

Weinberg understood this more clearly than anyone. A technology that differs too much from existing technology, he wrote, faces not one hurdle but two. The first is demonstrating that it works. The second is convincing people who are intellectually and emotionally invested in a different path to abandon it. The second hurdle, he concluded, is the larger one. He was right. He was also, by the time he wrote it, speaking from experience.

The nuclear case is one instance of a pattern. The next two are faster, more familiar, and closer to daily life — which is precisely why they matter.

---

In 1975, Sony introduced the Betamax. By almost every technical measure it was the superior product. It offered higher horizontal resolution, lower video noise, and less luminance/chrominance crosstalk than Video Home System (VHS) — the format its rival JVC had developed and which the world would come to know simply as VHS. The cassette was smaller and more elegant. Sony had invented a genuinely better format and expected the market to recognise it.

By the mid-1970s, Radio Corporation of America had long since stopped being primarily a radio company and was one of the largest consumer electronics and media conglomerates in the United States. When RCA examined early Betamax prototypes, its engineers asked Sony whether longer recording times were possible. Sony's response was that it could eventually be done, but picture quality would suffer and more development would be needed. Sony's marketing people reportedly told RCA to take the one-hour Betamax or leave it. They left it. Eighteen months later RCA went to JVC, whose VHS format already ran to two hours, and struck the deal that would define the next decade of consumer electronics. VHS launched in the United States in 1977, backed by RCA's manufacturing scale and marketing budget, with a format that could record an entire film on a single tape.

The race was not over yet, but its outcome was already structurally determined. The question was never again about picture quality. It was about recording time, then about price, then about which titles were available at the video rental store. Each of those questions had the same answer. By 1980, VHS controlled 60% of the North American market. By 1987, it accounted for 90%. Sony's engineers kept improving Betamax. They extended the recording time, reduced the price, and produced machines that outperformed VHS on every technical criterion their critics had raised. None of it mattered. The rental stores stocked what their customers owned. Customers bought what the rental stores stocked. The loop closed and Betamax was outside it.

In 1988, Sony conceded defeat and began manufacturing VHS players. The better format did not win. It was not suppressed or sabotaged. It simply arrived in a world where the infrastructure had organised itself around something else, and the cost of reorganising was higher than anyone was willing to pay.

This is what network effects look like from the inside. The technology that reaches critical mass first does not need to stay better. It only needs to stay good enough, for long enough, that switching becomes more expensive than tolerating its flaws. After a certain point, the question of which format was technically superior becomes genuinely irrelevant — not because anyone decided to ignore it, but because the infrastructure accumulated around VHS rendered the question moot. Sony's engineers were still right. It just didn't matter anymore.

---

At its peak in 2004, Blockbuster operated over 9,000 stores, employed 84,000 people, and generated six billion dollars in annual revenue. It was opening a new store somewhere in the world every seventeen hours. The company's moat was physical and seemingly impregnable — shelf space, distribution infrastructure, brand recognition built over two decades, and a customer base that had organised its Friday evenings around the ritual of the video rental store. When Netflix, a young mail-order DVD company founded in 1997, approached Blockbuster in 2000 with an offer to sell itself for fifty million dollars, Blockbuster's executives declined. Barry McCarthy, Netflix's former Chief Financial Officer, later recalled that Blockbuster had "laughed us out of their office."
Ten years later Blockbuster filed for bankruptcy with a billion dollars in debt. Today a single store remains, in Bend, Oregon, operating largely as a tourist attraction.

>Reed had the chutzpah to propose to them that we run their brand online and that they run our brand in the stores and they just about laughed us out of their office. At least initially, they thought we were a very small niche business.
>
>— Barry McCarthy: _CFO of Netflix, The Unofficial Stanford Blog, 2008_
{.source}

What happened between 2000 and 2010 was not a failure of execution. Blockbuster saw the threat, launched its own online rental service, and at one point was growing faster than Netflix. What it could not do was escape the weight of what it had already built. Nine thousand stores were not an asset in a world where the shelf had ceased to exist. They were a liability — expensive to maintain, impossible to shed quickly, and structurally incapable of competing with a company whose entire operation ran on servers and postal routes. The infrastructure that had made Blockbuster dominant made it immobile precisely when mobility was the only thing that mattered.
This is where the streaming case diverges from nuclear and from VHS, and where it becomes analytically useful. The lock-in broke. The question is why.

The answer lies in what the incumbent's moat was made of. Blockbuster's advantage was substrate-specific — it lived entirely in the physical world. Shelf space is a competitive moat only as long as the shelf is where people go to find things. The moment content became deliverable through a wire, Blockbuster's entire infrastructure became irrelevant, not merely inferior. Netflix did not have to beat Blockbuster at Blockbuster's own game. It changed the game entirely, and the old game's infrastructure was worthless in the new one.

Compare this with the nuclear case. The light water reactor's lock-in is not substrate-specific in the same way. The grid — the transmission lines, the regulatory frameworks, the trained workforce, the supply chains — does not become irrelevant when a better reactor design exists. It remains the grid. A molten salt reactor, however superior, still has to connect to the same infrastructure, navigate the same regulatory apparatus, and displace the same contractor relationships. There is no new delivery layer that renders the old one moot. The switching costs are civilisational in scale.
This suggests a rough but useful theory of lock-in brittleness. A lock-in is vulnerable when the incumbent's advantage is concentrated in a delivery layer that a new technology can bypass entirely. It is durable when the incumbent's advantage is embedded in infrastructure that the new technology must still use. Streaming could route around the video store. No reactor design can route around the grid.

---

The internal combustion engine has been the dominant technology in personal transport in India for over half a century. The infrastructure that grew around it is not merely large — it is civilisational. There are roughly 90,000 petrol stations across the country. The supply chains for engine components, transmission systems, and fuel delivery employ millions of people across dozens of industries. Mechanics are trained for it. Cities are zoned around it. The regulatory apparatus that governs vehicle manufacture, insurance, and resale assumes it. This is not a delivery layer. It is the substrate itself.

>Across roughly 800 districts, India averages to over a hundred petrol stations per district, in every corner of the country, from the industrial corridors of Gujarat to the hill districts of the northeast.

Electric vehicles are not routing around this infrastructure. They are attempting to displace it from within, while still depending on much of it — the roads, the regulatory frameworks, the manufacturing supply chains, the habits of hundreds of millions of drivers. The lock-in is breaking, visibly and unevenly. India sold 1.9 million electric vehicles in 2024, a 19% increase over the previous year, with electric two-wheelers and three-wheelers leading the transition. The trajectory is clear. The constraints are also clear.

India has stated a target of 30% electric vehicle share in all new vehicle sales by 2030. Reaching it will require at least 1.32 million public charging stations — roughly 400,000 new installations every year between now and then. As of late 2024, there were 25,202. There is currently one public charging station for every 235 electric vehicles on Indian roads. The investment required to close that gap by 2030 has been estimated at 2.36 billion US dollars. Between 2020 and 2025, roughly 9.6% of that amount was deployed. Eighty-two percent of the required capital remains unmet.

The grid compounds the problem. In several states, a lack of regional electric vehicle load forecasting hampers planning by utilities and agencies, creating the risk of grid bottlenecks as demand rises. Institute for Transportation and Development Policy The supply chains for battery components — lithium, cobalt, manganese — are geographically concentrated and underdeveloped at the scale mass adoption demands. These are not problems of will or investment alone. They are structural, compounding, and slow to resolve — precisely the properties that make a lock-in durable rather than brittle.

And yet the Indian transition does not fit cleanly into the durable category either. Unlike the nuclear case, a partial new delivery layer already exists — the electrical grid reaches most of the country, and the dominance of two-wheelers and three-wheelers means the energy requirements per vehicle are far lower than in markets built around cars. Unlike VHS, no single corporate decision sealed the outcome early. The lock-in is being contested by a combination of policy, falling battery costs, and the specific geometry of Indian mobility that the internal combustion engine's incumbency cannot fully resist.
The question is not whether it breaks. It is how long the breaking takes, and what calcifies in the meantime.

Every year the internal combustion engine remains dominant is another year of petrol stations built, engine factories tooled, mechanics trained, and habits formed. The infrastructure does not wait for the transition to complete before deepening. Path dependence does not pause while better alternatives gather momentum. It compounds quietly, in planning decisions and supply contracts and zoning codes, until the moment when the question of which technology was better becomes the same irrelevant question it became for Betamax, for the molten salt reactor, and for every other good idea that arrived after the infrastructure had already decided.

We are inside that moment now. The outcome is not settled. But the clock has been running for a long time.
